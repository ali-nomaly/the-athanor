Sourdough

A rich, hearty loaf — the way it's meant to be
🌾

Yields 1 large loaf  ·  About 24 hours total  ·  Feeds 6–8

This is the kind of bread that fills a kitchen with warmth and takes over the whole day in the best way. No shortcuts — just flour, water, salt, and a living starter doing what they've done for thousands of years. Dark crust. Chewy crumb. The real thing.

The Method
Step by Step
01
Mix
Autolyse — Let the flour wake up
⏱ 30–60 minutes
In a large bowl, combine the flour and 300g of the water (hold back 25g). Mix with your hands until no dry flour remains — it'll be shaggy and rough, that's perfect. Cover and rest for 30–60 minutes. This rest hydrates the flour and starts building gluten with zero effort.
Don't skip this rest. It makes the dough dramatically easier to work and the crumb more open.
02
Build
Add starter, salt & oil
⏱ 10 minutes
Pour the active starter over the dough. Dissolve the salt in the remaining 25g water and add it too. Add the olive oil. Squeeze and fold everything together with your hands — it'll feel slippery and messy for a minute, then it'll come together. Work it until fully incorporated.
Adding salt and starter separately gives you more control over fermentation timing. Salt after starter is the classic method.
03
Fold
Stretch & fold — build the structure
⏱ 4 sets over 2 hours
Every 30 minutes for the first 2 hours, do one set of stretch & folds: wet your hand, grab one side of the dough, stretch it up as high as it'll go without tearing, and fold it over the center. Rotate the bowl 90° and repeat — four stretches total per set. 4 sets, then leave it alone.
This is your workout and the dough's workout. Each set you'll feel it getting stronger, smoother, and more elastic.
04
Rise
Bulk fermentation — let it live
⏱ 4–6 hours at room temp
Cover the bowl and leave it at room temperature (ideally 75–78°F). The dough is done with bulk when it's grown 50–75%, looks domed and jiggly, and you can see bubbles on the sides. In a warm kitchen this takes 4 hours. In a cool one, up to 6 or more. Trust the dough, not the clock.
Warmer kitchen = faster ferment. Cooler = slower and usually more flavor. Both work. Watch the dough, not the clock.
05
Shape
Shape into a boule
⏱ 10–15 minutes
Flour your counter lightly. Gently turn the dough out — don't punch it down. Using a bench scraper, fold the edges toward the center all around, then flip it seam-side down. Cup your hands around the back and drag it toward you to build surface tension. It should feel taut and round. Let it rest 20 minutes uncovered (bench rest), then do a final shape: fold edges to center again, flip seam-down into a floured banneton (or a bowl lined with a well-floured kitchen towel).
Semolina or rice flour in the banneton prevents sticking far better than bread flour.
06
Proof
Cold proof overnight
⏱ 8–16 hours in the fridge
Cover the banneton with plastic wrap or a shower cap and refrigerate overnight — or up to 16 hours. The cold slows fermentation way down and develops deep, complex flavor. This is also what makes the dough easier to score cleanly. You can bake it straight from the fridge.
This is where patience pays you back in flavor. Longer cold proof = more tang and complexity.
07
Bake
Bake in a hot Dutch oven
⏱ 20 min covered + 25–30 min uncovered
Place your Dutch oven in the oven and preheat to 500°F (260°C) for at least 45 minutes — it must be scorching hot. Turn the cold dough out onto parchment, score the top with a razor or sharp knife at a 45° angle (one confident slash, ½ inch deep). Lift by the parchment into the Dutch oven. Cover and bake 20 minutes. Remove lid, reduce to 450°F, bake another 25–30 minutes until the crust is deeply dark — don't be shy. Dark crust = deep flavor.
The covered phase traps steam so the bread springs up. The uncovered phase builds the crust. Both matter equally.
08
Rest
The hardest step: wait
⏱ At least 1 hour, ideally 2
Pull the loaf from the Dutch oven and set it on a wire rack. Do not cut it. The crumb is still setting — cutting it hot makes it gummy and ruins everything you've built. Let it cool fully. You'll hear it crackle and sing as the crust settles. That sound is the reward.
Waiting is genuinely the hardest part of this whole process. It's also non-negotiable. The bread needs this.
When Things Go Sideways
Troubleshooting
🧱 Dense, heavy crumb
Starter wasn't active enough, or bulk ferment was too short. Your starter should float in water when it's ready.
🫓 Flat loaf, no oven spring
Over-fermented — the dough went past its peak. In warm weather, reduce bulk time or use less starter.
😶 No sour flavor
Longer cold proof = more tang. Try 16 hours instead of 8. A cooler room during bulk also builds more acid.
🔪 Score blew out weird
Score quickly and confidently at 45°. Dull blade drags and tears. Use a lame or a brand-new razor blade.
💧 Dough too sticky to work
Wet hands always — never add more flour. Sticky is normal. The stretch & folds will tighten it up as gluten builds.
🌑 Crust too pale
Bake longer uncovered, or push oven temp higher. A pale crust almost always means underbaked. Go darker than you think.
