The Map
Of Prana

The five vayus and three nadis — where yogic energy anatomy and the autonomic nervous system describe the same machinery in different vocabularies. The convergence here is the most precise on the archive.

// PREMISE

The yogic seers who composed the Upanishads and the Hatha Yoga Pradipika did not have electrodes. They had attention. They sat with breath for thousands of hours and described what they observed in the only language available to them — direction, current, channel, fire, moon.

What they mapped is the same nervous system we now describe with monitors. The correspondences are not approximate. Ida is parasympathetic. Pingala is sympathetic. The five vayus are functional flow patterns the autonomic system orchestrates. The map is precise. Only the vocabulary differs.

// FIG.01 — THE_THREE_NADIS

The Three Channels

Nadis are the energetic currents of the subtle body — described in yogic literature as 72,000 in number, but three are primary. Click each channel to read its dual mapping: yogic description and modern physiological correlate.

[ NADI_01 // LUNAR_CHANNEL ]
Ida
The cooling, receptive, lunar current. Begins at the left nostril, terminates at the base of the spine.

// YOGIC FRAME

  • Lunar (chandra)
  • Cooling, receptive
  • Left-side dominance
  • Feminine principle (shakti)
  • Mind / mental introversion
  • Quiescence, rest, intuition

// NEURAL FRAME

  • Parasympathetic dominance
  • Vagal tone elevation
  • Right-hemisphere activity
  • Rest-digest cascade
  • HRV-coherent state
  • Default mode integration

When ida is dominant — observable in left-nostril patency during nasal cycle monitoring — the system is in a parasympathetic-biased state. Cooler skin temperature on the right side of the body. Slower heart rate. Digestion engaged. Mental introversion.

Yogic practice that emphasizes left-nostril breathing (chandra bhedana) deliberately activates this current — and consistent with the mapping, it lowers blood pressure, slows heart rate, and shifts hemispheric balance toward the right.

// FIG.02 — THE_FIVE_VAYUS

The Five Currents

Vayu means "wind" — a directional flow of prana through the body. Each governs a specific region and function. Together they describe what modern physiology calls the autonomic orchestration of organ systems. Select a vayu to explore its mapping.

[ VAYU_01 // INTAKE_CURRENT ]
Prana
SEAT: Chest / Heart // DIRECTION: Inward, Receiving
// LOCATION

Thoracic region. Lungs, heart, the receiving cavity of the breath.

// FUNCTION

The taking-in of breath, food, sensory impression. The principle of intake.

Prana vayu is, confusingly, both the general name for life-force and one specific vayu — the receiving current. It governs inhalation, the heartbeat that pulls blood in, the eyes and ears as receivers of sensory data.

When prana vayu is balanced, intake is clean — you can breathe deeply, receive nourishment, take in experience without forcing or refusing. When disrupted, you see shallow breath, sensory overwhelm, or the opposite — sensory shutdown.

// FILE.03 — THE_INTEGRATED_STATE

When the Channels Equalize

The yogic claim: when breath in left and right nostrils is exactly balanced, prana enters the central channel — sushumna. The modern claim: when sympathetic and parasympathetic harmonize, the system enters coherence. They are pointing at the same state.

"When both nostrils flow equally, the central channel opens, and the mind moves toward yoga."

— PARAPHRASED FROM THE HATHA YOGA PRADIPIKA, 15TH C.

The yogis observed something verifiable: the nasal cycle. Roughly every 90-150 minutes, dominance shifts between left and right nostrils. This is now understood as autonomic — the swelling and shrinking of nasal turbinates is controlled by sympathetic/parasympathetic balance.

When the cycle transitions, there is a brief window where both nostrils flow equally. The yogic literature claims this is when meditative states deepen most readily — when the central channel is open. Modern measurement: this is also when HRV is highest and autonomic balance is most coherent.

The practice of nadi shodhana (alternate nostril breathing) is, in this frame, a deliberate engineering of that balance state — forcing ida and pingala into equal flow until the system finds its central channel. The yogis prescribed it for spiritual reasons. The neurophysiology confirms it works on the autonomic balance they were describing.

This is the heart of the convergence: the integrated nervous system state — where neither branch of the autonomic dominates, where the pilot rests in the Eva without override — is what the yogis called sushumna. The map is the same. Only the names differ.

// ADVISORY // TELEOLOGY_PROTOCOL

On Working With the Map

The map of prana is a diagnostic instrument. Each vayu and nadi can be in excess, deficiency, or balance — and the question to ask is always Adlerian: what is this pattern in service of?

Chronic ida dominance — left-nostril patency, parasympathetic over-tone — often presents as withdrawal, lethargy, dissociation. The pattern can be in service of safety through invisibility — a strategy for an environment where presence felt dangerous. Pranayama to balance the system without addressing the underlying goal will not hold.

Chronic pingala dominance — right-nostril patency, sympathetic excess — often presents as drive, vigilance, restlessness. The pattern can be in service of safety through productivity — a strategy for an environment where rest meant losing position. Calming practices alone will be experienced as threatening if the pilot has not yet examined the goal the activation serves.

The vayus are similar. Compulsive samana imbalance (digestive obsession, perfectionism around food) may serve a goal of control over the uncontrollable. Disrupted apana (chronic holding, inability to release) may serve not letting go of what is unsafe to release.

The technique addresses the symptom. The teleological inquiry addresses the goal. Both are needed. Neither replaces the other.
