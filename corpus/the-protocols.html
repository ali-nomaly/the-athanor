The previous archives describe the bridge, the map, the mechanism, the catalogue. This one is active. The instrument below paces breath in real time, configured by whichever protocol the pilot selects. Six protocols address six states the system actually encounters — morning lethargy, pre-sleep activation, acute stress, focus before deep work, post-stress dysregulation, and meditative anchor.

The protocols are not magic. They are sequenced applications of techniques already specified elsewhere in this archive. Their power is in being ready when needed, configured correctly, and applied with clear teleology. Begin by selecting one.

Six Protocols

Each protocol is a sequenced application of one or more techniques from the catalogue, configured for a specific state. Click load protocol to send its rhythm to the pacer above — the page scrolls back to the instrument, the rhythm preloads, and the protocol is ready to begin.

[ PROTOCOL_01 // ACTIVATION ]

Morning Activation

// WHEN: ON WAKING · LOW-ENERGY MORNINGS
TECHNIQUESURYA-LIKE PATTERN RHYTHM4 · 0 · 4 · 0 ROUNDS12 DURATION~01:36

An even, energizing rhythm — equal inhale and exhale with no holds, paced briskly. Modeled on Surya Bhedana's intent without the nostril discipline (which can come later when the eyes are properly open). Wakes the system through controlled sympathetic activation.

Sit upright if possible. Resist the temptation to do this while still in bed — the body needs to feel that the day has begun.

Serves waking the pilot. Avoid if the morning grogginess is genuine sleep debt — that calls for more sleep, not more activation.
CALM
[ PROTOCOL_02 // WIND_DOWN ]

Pre-Sleep Wind-Down

// WHEN: LYING IN BED · BEFORE SLEEP
TECHNIQUE4-7-8 PATTERN RHYTHM4 · 7 · 8 · 0 ROUNDS6 DURATION~01:54

The doubled exhale is the active ingredient. The 7-count hold builds mild CO₂ accumulation that deepens the subsequent release. The system arrives at the parasympathetic downshift the sleep state requires.

Lie down. Lights low. Don't strain the counts — adjust the pace so the rhythm feels natural, only the ratio matters.

Serves downshifting into sleep. If sleep doesn't come, the protocol is not the issue — the day's signals are. Worth listening to what is keeping you awake before sedating it.
RESET
[ PROTOCOL_03 // INTERRUPT ]

Acute Stress Interrupt

// WHEN: SPIKED · OVERWHELMED · MOMENT-TO-MOMENT
TECHNIQUEPHYSIOLOGICAL SIGH RHYTHM2 + 1 · 6 (audible) ROUNDS3 DURATION~00:27

The fastest known physiological off-ramp. Two stacked inhales fully open the alveoli; the long audible exhale through the mouth dumps accumulated CO₂ and engages the parasympathetic. Three rounds is usually enough.

Can be done sitting at a desk, walking, mid-conversation if needed. The most operationally usable practice in this archive.

Serves acute interruption — bringing the system back into range so the pilot can think clearly about what just happened. Not a substitute for examining what spiked the system.
FOCUS
[ PROTOCOL_04 // PRE-WORK ]

Focus Before Deep Work

// WHEN: BEFORE FOCUSED WORK · DIFFICULT CONVERSATIONS
TECHNIQUEBOX BREATHING RHYTHM4 · 4 · 4 · 4 ROUNDS10 DURATION~02:40

The equal four-part rhythm balances both branches of the autonomic system while the deliberate counting recruits prefrontal attention. The mild CO₂ load during the holds builds tolerance and engages the diving response slightly — sharpening focus without sedation.

Sit at the workspace. Don't multitask the practice. Two and a half minutes of nothing else.

Serves steady alertness — present without being activated. The protocol most easily integrated into a working day. Operates cleanly because it tilts neither way.
CALM
[ PROTOCOL_05 // RECOVERY ]

Post-Stress Recovery

// WHEN: AFTER A SPIKE · INTEGRATION TIME
TECHNIQUENADI SHODHANA RHYTHM4 · 4 · 4 · 4 (alt-nostril) ROUNDS9 DURATION~02:24

After acute activation has been interrupted (Protocol 03), the system still benefits from active rebalancing. Alternate nostril breathing engineers the equalization of ida and pingala — pulling the autonomic toward coherence rather than just dropping it back to baseline.

Sit. Use the right hand traditionally — thumb on right nostril, ring finger on left, the timing of switches as the pacer marks phase transitions. Follow the rhythm; the nostril discipline you handle.

Serves re-integration after the system has been thrown. The difference from a simple calming protocol: this one re-balances, rather than merely suppressing.
FOCUS
[ PROTOCOL_06 // ANCHOR ]

Meditative Anchor

// WHEN: SITTING PRACTICE · SUSTAINED PRESENCE
TECHNIQUECOHERENT BREATHING RHYTHM5.5 · 0 · 5.5 · 0 ROUNDS30 DURATION~05:30

Resonance breathing at ~6 breaths per minute. Equal inhale and exhale of ~5.5 seconds each, no holds. At this rate, HRV peaks and the cardiovascular and respiratory systems phase-lock. The pacer's rhythm becomes a soft external anchor for attention — held loosely, not gripped.

Sit cross-legged or upright in a chair. Use the visual as anchor, then optionally let go of it and continue without watching. Practice itself, not technique.

Serves sustained presence. The closest thing to a baseline regulating practice — the one most safely repeated daily without distortion of intent.
// ADVISORY // TELEOLOGY_PROTOCOL

On the Active Practice

The pacer is operational. The protocols are loaded. The inquiry remains.

The risk specific to this page — the active practice page — is the seduction of the instrument itself. The pacer is beautifully responsive. The protocols are clearly delineated. The telemetry counts up reliably. None of this is the practice.

The practice is what happens in the body while the pacer runs. The widget is a metronome. The metronome does not play the music. The pilot does.

Some sessions will follow the pacer perfectly and produce nothing. Others will go off-rhythm in the second round and produce a profound moment. The instrument optimizes for adherence to a pattern. The transformation optimizes for something else — the willingness to be in contact with what arises while the pattern runs.

Use the protocols. Use the pacer. Stay with what they invoke, not with the metrics they generate.
