The
Techniques

Twelve pranayama practices specified by mechanism, rhythm, and intended effect. Filter by state. Each card includes the Adlerian inquiry — what is this in service of, before it is what it is.

// PREMISE

A catalogue is not a curriculum. The techniques below are not a sequence to work through — they are a set of levers, each engineered to produce a specific physiological and felt effect. The skilled practitioner does not memorize them all and apply them randomly. They learn the small subset that serves the states they actually need to engineer, and they keep the rest available for when the need arises.

Every technique here has a clear mechanism and an intended outcome. None is universally "good for you." The question is always: what state does my system require, and which lever produces it?

[ TECH_01 // BALANCING ]
नाडी शोधन

Nadi Shodhana

"channel cleansing" — alternate nostril breath
RHYTHM 4·4·4·4 L-NOSTRIL → R-NOSTRIL
// FUNCTION
The keystone balancing practice. Alternating inhalation and exhalation between nostrils deliberately equalizes ida and pingala, the lunar and solar channels — drawing the system toward sushumna, the central channel of integration.
Alternate nostril breathing has been shown to balance hemispheric activity, raise HRV, lower blood pressure, and bring the autonomic nervous system into measurable coherence within 5-10 minutes of practice.
  1. Right hand to face. Right thumb closes right nostril.
  2. Inhale through left nostril (4 count).
  3. Ring finger closes left. Release right. Exhale right (4).
  4. Inhale right (4). Close right. Exhale left (4).
  5. This is one round. Begin at 5 rounds. Build to 10-15.
Used in service of integration: re-finding the center after a fragmenting day. If used compulsively to feel "balanced," it can mask the question of what unbalanced the system in the first place.

[ TECH_02 // VAGAL_VIBRATION ]
भ्रामरी

Bhramari

"bee breath" — humming exhalation
RHYTHM 4·8~HUM NASAL · CLOSED EYES
// FUNCTION
Slow nasal inhale followed by a long, audible humming exhale. The vibration travels through the skull, the throat, and the chest. Strongly parasympathetic-engaging and uniquely effective at quieting cortical chatter.
Humming increases nasal nitric oxide output ~15-fold (Weitzberg & Lundberg, 2002). The laryngeal vibration directly stimulates the vagus through its recurrent laryngeal branch. The lengthened exhale further engages baroreceptor reflex pathways.
  1. Sit comfortably. Eyes closed. Optionally, fingers lightly over ears.
  2. Inhale slowly through nose (4 count).
  3. Exhale with a steady, low "mmmm" humming sound (8+ count).
  4. Feel the vibration in the skull, throat, chest.
  5. 5-10 rounds. Best before sleep or in moments of cortical overload.
Used in service of quieting — and unusually effective for it. Worth examining if the quieting is sought to integrate experience, or to mute a signal that should be heard.

[ TECH_03 // RESONANCE ]
[ modern / clinical ]

Coherent Breathing

resonance breathing — ~6 breaths per minute
RHYTHM 5.5·5.5 ~6 BR/MIN
// FUNCTION
The simplest possible practice: equal inhale and exhale, around five and a half seconds each, producing approximately six breaths per minute. At this rate, heart rate variability peaks. The cardiovascular and respiratory systems enter resonance.
The 0.1 Hz resonant frequency of the baroreflex aligns with ~6 breaths/min. At this rate, blood pressure oscillation, breathing, and HRV all phase-lock — producing maximum autonomic coherence with minimum effort.
  1. No special preparation required.
  2. Inhale through nose for ~5.5 seconds.
  3. Exhale through nose for ~5.5 seconds.
  4. No retention. No effort. Just the slow steady rhythm.
  5. Begin with 5 minutes. Extend to 20 if desired.
Used in service of baseline regulation. The closest thing to a universally beneficial practice. Almost no failure modes — but precisely because of that, can be relied on as a way to manage symptoms without engaging causes.

[ TECH_04 // EXTENDED_EXHALE ]
[ modern / clinical ]

4 · 7 · 8

inhale 4 · hold 7 · exhale 8
RHYTHM 4·7·8 FOR FALLING ASLEEP
// FUNCTION
Popularized by Andrew Weil, drawing from yogic retention practice. A short inhale, moderate retention, and doubled exhale. The 8-count exhale is the active ingredient — it surges parasympathetic engagement reliably enough to be used clinically for sleep induction.
The lengthened exhale (twice the inhale) reliably triggers vagal afferent firing via baroreceptors and pulmonary stretch receptors. The hold builds mild CO₂ accumulation, deepening the subsequent release.
  1. Inhale silently through nose for 4 counts.
  2. Hold the breath for 7 counts.
  3. Exhale through pursed lips (audible) for 8 counts.
  4. 4 cycles maximum at first. Lying in bed.
  5. Effect typically reached within 2-3 cycles.
Used in service of downshifting at the threshold of sleep. Effective and benign. Becomes worth examining if needed nightly as the only way to fall asleep — that is the system telling you something about the day, not about the technique.

[ TECH_05 // FASTEST_RESET ]
[ modern / Feinberg, Huberman ]

Physiological Sigh

double-inhale + long exhale
RHYTHM IN·IN+·OUT--- 1-3 REPS
// FUNCTION
Identified by Mark Krasnow's lab as the spontaneous reset the body performs roughly every five minutes. Two inhales (the second a top-up) followed by a long, audible exhale. Resets blood gas balance and downregulates arousal faster than any other known breath pattern.
The double inhale fully expands alveoli that have collapsed during normal breathing, optimizing gas exchange surface area. The extended exhale offloads accumulated CO₂ rapidly while engaging parasympathetic pathways.
  1. Through the nose: inhale to about 80% lung capacity.
  2. Without exhaling, take a second short sharp inhale on top.
  3. Exhale slowly and audibly through the mouth — empty.
  4. 1-3 repetitions is typically sufficient.
  5. The fastest physiological off-ramp from acute stress.
Used in service of acute interruption — when the nervous system has spiked and needs to land. Among the most teleologically clean practices: no altered state, no claim, just a reset. The thing the body would have done on its own had it been allowed.

[ TECH_06 // LUNAR ]
चन्द्र भेदन

Chandra Bhedana

"moon-piercing breath" — left nostril only
RHYTHM 4·8 L-IN · R-OUT
// FUNCTION
Inhale exclusively through the left nostril (ida); exhale through the right (pingala). Strongly activates the cooling, parasympathetic lunar current while expelling residual sympathetic activation through the solar channel.
Forced left-nostril breathing has been shown in studies to reduce blood pressure, lower heart rate, and shift cortical activity toward right-hemisphere dominance — consistent with the yogic mapping of ida to parasympathetic dominance.
  1. Close right nostril with right thumb.
  2. Inhale slowly through left nostril (4 count).
  3. Close left nostril, open right.
  4. Exhale through right nostril (8 count).
  5. 9-12 rounds. Avoid in cold or low-energy states.
Used in service of cooling — useful in heat, anger, agitation. Worth checking the pattern: chronic preference for chandra over surya can serve a goal of avoiding activation, which is different from genuinely calibrating it.

[ TECH_07 // BELLOWS ]
भस्त्रिका

Bhastrika

"bellows breath" — forceful in and out
RHYTHM ~1/SEC·FORCEFUL SYMPATHETIC
// FUNCTION
Strong, forceful, equal-pressure inhale and exhale through the nose — the diaphragm pumps like a blacksmith's bellows. Powerfully sympathetic-activating, generating heat, mental clarity, and physiological alertness rapidly.
The active engagement of both inhalation and exhalation (unlike Kapalabhati's passive inhale) recruits the full diaphragmatic and intercostal pump. Drops CO₂, raises adrenaline, increases cortical arousal. Effectively an internal cardio.
  1. Sit upright. Hands on knees or lower belly.
  2. Forceful inhale through nose, forceful exhale — equal effort both directions.
  3. Pace: about one breath per second.
  4. 20-30 breaths. Pause. Breathe naturally. Repeat 2-3 rounds.
  5. Discontinue immediately if lightheaded.
Used in service of activation — bypassing morning lethargy, building heat in cold practice spaces. Worth scrutinizing if pursued for the altered state itself, which is often hypoxia-adjacent and can be confused with insight.

[ TECH_08 // SKULL_SHINING ]
कपालभाति

Kapalabhati

"skull-shining breath" — sharp exhales, passive inhales
RHYTHM SHARP-OUT·PASSIVE-IN ~2/SEC
// FUNCTION
Sharp, percussive exhales through the nose driven by the lower abdomen; inhale is entirely passive, the natural rebound of the abdominal release. Generates heat, sharpens mental clarity, "polishes the skull" of stagnation.
Classified as a kriya (cleansing) rather than strictly pranayama. The rhythmic abdominal pumping massages internal organs and lymph nodes, while the rapid CO₂ offload produces a transient hyperventilation-like alertness.
  1. Sit tall. Hand on low belly to feel the action.
  2. Sharp contraction of low belly drives air out through nose.
  3. Release belly; inhale happens automatically.
  4. Pace: 60-120 pumps per round.
  5. 1-3 rounds. Rest fully between rounds.
Used in service of burning off stagnation — mental fog, residual lethargy. Pursued compulsively, can become a way of blowing past emotions rather than meeting them. The cleansing intent matters.

[ TECH_09 // SOLAR ]
सूर्य भेदन

Surya Bhedana

"sun-piercing breath" — right nostril only
RHYTHM 4·4 R-IN · L-OUT
// FUNCTION
The polar complement of Chandra Bhedana: inhale exclusively through right nostril (pingala); exhale through left (ida). Activates the warming, sympathetic solar current. Used to wake the system, generate heat, build outward focus.
Right-nostril forced breathing has been measured to raise heart rate, increase body temperature, raise oxygen consumption, and shift cortical balance toward the left hemisphere — consistent with sympathetic activation and pingala dominance.
  1. Close left nostril with right ring finger.
  2. Inhale slowly through right nostril (4 count).
  3. Close right, open left.
  4. Exhale through left nostril (4 count).
  5. 9-12 rounds. Avoid in anxious or already-activated states.
Used in service of warming the system — winter mornings, lethargy, depressive flatness. Worth examining: if reached for as an escape from stillness, it serves a different goal than calibration.

[ TECH_10 // EQUAL_RATIO ]
[ modern / tactical ]

Box Breathing

sama vritti — equal four-part breath
RHYTHM 4·4·4·4 IN · HOLD · OUT · HOLD
// FUNCTION
Four equal phases: inhale, hold, exhale, hold — typically four counts each. Used by Navy SEALs for pre-action focus, and by yogic practitioners as sama vritti — "even fluctuation." Builds steady, alert presence without either activating or sedating.
The equal four-part rhythm balances sympathetic and parasympathetic input, while the deliberate counting recruits prefrontal attention. CO₂ levels rise mildly during the holds, building tolerance and engaging the diving response slightly.
  1. Inhale through nose for 4 counts.
  2. Hold the breath (gentle, not strained) for 4 counts.
  3. Exhale through nose for 4 counts.
  4. Hold empty for 4 counts.
  5. Continue 5-10 minutes. Increase count as comfort builds.
Used in service of steady alertness — before difficult conversations, focused work, decisions under pressure. Among the most operationally clean techniques — neither over-stimulating nor sedating, simply present.

[ TECH_11 // OCEANIC ]
उज्जायी

Ujjayi

"victorious breath" — slight throat constriction
RHYTHM SLOW·EVEN·AUDIBLE CONTINUOUS
// FUNCTION
A slight constriction at the glottis creates audible turbulent airflow — the "oceanic" sound — through both inhale and exhale. Sustains focus through self-generated sensory feedback; foundational to asana practice and meditative concentration.
Glottal constriction increases airway resistance, requiring deeper diaphragmatic engagement. The audible feedback recruits attention continuously. Engages the same laryngeal-vagal pathway as humming, contributing to parasympathetic activation despite the practice's alerting effect.
  1. Slight constriction at the back of the throat — as if fogging a mirror with mouth closed.
  2. Inhale slowly through nose — audible oceanic sound.
  3. Exhale through nose with the same gentle throat hold — audible sound.
  4. Continue smoothly. Use throughout asana practice or meditation.
  5. Sound is soft, internal — others should barely hear it.
Used in service of sustained presence — the breath that anchors movement, sustains concentration through long sits. Almost no failure modes when held correctly; the rare risk is forcing the sound and creating throat tension.

[ TECH_12 // CONSCIOUS_CONNECTED ]
[ Grof / Orr / Holotropic ]

Connected Breathing

rebirthing / holotropic / circular breath
RHYTHM NO-PAUSE·MOUTH·FULL 30-60 MIN SESSIONS
// FUNCTION
Continuous, full inhalation and exhalation through the mouth with no pause between breaths. Sustained for extended periods, produces altered states involving emotional release, somatic catharsis, and non-ordinary consciousness. The most powerful — and most dangerous — practice in the catalogue.
Sustained over-breathing drops blood CO₂ significantly, producing respiratory alkalosis. The Bohr effect shifts — hemoglobin clings to oxygen, cerebral hypoxia develops, and the brain enters altered states characterized by intense emotional content, body sensations, and dissolution of ordinary cognitive boundaries.
  1. This technique requires preparation, time, and ideally a trained facilitator.
  2. Lying down, eyes closed, ideally with evocative music.
  3. Full, deep, mouth-breathing — no pause between inhale and exhale.
  4. Sustained for 30-60+ minutes. Tetany, tears, tremors common.
  5. Integration period afterward is non-negotiable.
The teleological inquiry here is critical. Connected breathing can serve genuine integration of stored emotional material — and can also serve repetitive catharsis as a substitute for change. The altered state is, mechanistically, induced cerebral hypoxia. The content that surfaces is real; the technique is real. The question is what the system is using the practice for. Repeated catharsis without integration is not the same as transformation. Strongly inadvisable for those with cardiovascular conditions, psychosis history, or unprocessed trauma without skilled support.
// ADVISORY // TELEOLOGY_PROTOCOL

On Choosing a Technique

The catalogue invites a question more interesting than "which technique should I do today?" The Adlerian question is: what state am I attempting to engineer, and why?

If the system is genuinely dysregulated and a specific lever brings it back to baseline — calm when activated, alert when foggy, present when scattered — the technique has served its purpose. This is the use case the techniques were built for.

But there is a second use case that often masquerades as the first. The system is uncomfortable not because it is dysregulated but because it is reporting something true. Discomfort can be a signal that conditions need to change, that a relationship needs naming, that a direction is wrong. The technique applied to this kind of discomfort serves a different goal — silencing the signal so the conditions need not be changed. The system returns to baseline. The cause persists.

Both uses produce the same physiological result. They are distinguished only by the inquiry: what is this in service of? Asked before each session — not as moral test, but as diagnostic instrument — the question keeps the techniques aligned with what the pilot actually needs, rather than allowing them to become a sophisticated way of not feeling what is present.

The techniques are excellent. They are also only ever instruments. The lever does not pull itself, and the lever does not know what it should be in service of. Only the pilot does.
