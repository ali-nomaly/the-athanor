01

The Keyboard Layout

C notes — the anchors of the keyboard
White keys — natural notes (A B C D E F G)
Black keys — sharps (♯) & flats (♭)

The keyboard repeats the same 12-note pattern (7 white + 5 black) over and over. Each repetition is called an octave. Middle C (C4) sits roughly in the center of a full 88-key piano and is your primary home base. Notice the black key clusters — groups of 2 and groups of 3 — they are your visual landmarks. The white key just to the left of any group of 2 black keys is always C.

The black keys are named after their neighbors. C♯ = D♭ (same key, two names). Context (the key signature of a piece) determines which name you use. Sharps go up (♯), flats go down (♭). There is no black key between E and F, or between B and C — these are natural half steps, and they're essential to understand.

03

Finger Numbering

Left Hand (LH)
Right Hand (RH)

For the Right Hand: Thumb = 1, Index = 2, Middle = 3, Ring = 4, Pinky = 5. For the Left Hand: the thumbs are still 1 — but since the hands mirror each other on the keys, the pinky on your left is finger 5 and sits on the left side. Sheet music uses these numbers (called fingering) to tell you which fingers to use. Correct fingering = efficient motion = speed.

04

Posture & Hand Position

🪑
Seat Height
Sit so your elbows are at or slightly above key level. Feet flat on the floor. Don't hunch — the power comes from relaxed arms, not hunching.
🤲
Hand Shape
Imagine holding a small orange. Your knuckles should be the highest point, fingers gently curved, wrists floating level — not collapsed or arched.
💪
Arm Weight
Don't press with tension — drop the weight of your arm into the key. The key resists; gravity does the work. Tension is the enemy of speed.
👆
Fingertips
Play on the fleshy pads of your fingertips, not the nails. Shorter nails help — they force you into the correct position naturally.
🔄
Wrist Motion
The wrist acts as a shock absorber. It can rotate and flex slightly — but it should never become a pivot point or lock up.
👁️
Eyes Off Keys
Train yourself to feel the geography of the keys without looking. Start slow. Muscle memory of key spacing is what allows you to play intuitively.
05

Scales & The Major Scale Formula

C Major Scale
W · W · H · W · W · W · H  (W = Whole Step / H = Half Step)
G Major Scale
Same formula — one sharp (F♯)
F Major Scale
Same formula — one flat (B♭)

Every major scale uses the same interval formula: W-W-H-W-W-W-H. A whole step (W) skips one key; a half step (H) is the very next key. Learn C major on white keys first — then apply the formula starting from any note to find that major scale. The pattern is always the same; only the starting pitch changes.


Right hand fingering for C major: 1-2-3 (thumb under) 1-2-3-4-5 going up. Left hand: 5-4-3-2-1 (finger over) 3-2-1 going up.

06

Basic Chords

A triad (3-note chord) is built by stacking thirds: take a root note, skip one, take the next, skip one, take the next. Major triads sound bright: root + 4 half steps + 3 half steps. Minor triads sound darker: root + 3 half steps + 4 half steps. Learn to build and play these from any note — this is the bedrock of chord-based intuitive playing.

In 4/4 time (the most common), there are 4 beats per measure and a quarter note gets 1 beat. Use a metronome every single practice session — internal rhythm is a skill, and it's built through repetition with a steady pulse. Start slower than feels comfortable. Slow and accurate beats fast and messy every time.

09

Your Practice Roadmap

Phase 01
Orientation & Posture
  • Learn where every white key is by feel, without looking
  • Practice the correct hand shape (curved fingers, floating wrist)
  • Play each finger independently: 1-2-3-4-5 slowly on white keys
  • 5-finger position: place RH thumb on C, play C-D-E-F-G and back
Weeks 1–2
Phase 02
Notes, Scales & Simple Melodies
  • Memorize note names on all white keys across 2 octaves
  • Learn C major scale with proper fingering, both hands separately
  • Play simple melodies (Ode to Joy, Mary Had a Little Lamb) by ear
  • Begin using a metronome at 60 BPM
  • Introduce G major and F major scales
Weeks 3–6
Phase 03
Chords & Both Hands
  • Learn C, G, Am, F chords (the "four-chord song" foundation)
  • Practice hands separately first, then together slowly
  • Simple LH pattern: bass note + chord (Alberti bass or broken chord)
  • Play chord progressions with a metronome
  • Learn chord inversions for smooth voice leading
Weeks 7–12
Phase 04
Reading Music & Repertoire
  • Learn treble and bass clef note reading (use mnemonics)
  • Pick one beginner piece you love and learn it measure by measure
  • Practice sight-reading easy pieces daily (even 5 minutes counts)
  • Explore dynamics: piano (soft), forte (loud), crescendo
Months 3–5
Phase 05
Toward Intuitive Playing
  • Learn the 12 major scales and their relative minors
  • Practice chord progressions in all 12 keys
  • Improvise over a single chord — just explore sound
  • Learn the pentatonic scale (5 notes = forgiving improv tool)
  • Transcribe simple melodies by ear — this builds your inner ear
  • Study songs you love: what chords? What patterns?
Months 6–12+
10

Principles for Effective Practice

01
Slow Is Fast
Always learn something new at a tempo where you make zero mistakes. Your hands learn what you repeat — including errors. Slow perfect > fast sloppy.
02
Isolate Trouble Spots
Don't run through an entire piece. Find the measure that trips you up and loop just that. 10x on the hard part beats 1x on the whole thing.
03
Hands Separately First
Always learn the right hand, then the left hand, then put them together. Skipping to both-hands too quickly builds bad habits.
04
Daily Over Long Sessions
20 minutes every day beats 3 hours once a week. Motor memory is built through consistent repetition over time, not marathon sessions.
05
Listen Actively
Listen to music with intention — identify chords, note how tension resolves, notice rhythmic patterns. Your ear teaches your hands.
06
End with Joy
Always end your practice session with something you can already play well. This reinforces confidence and keeps your relationship with the instrument positive.
