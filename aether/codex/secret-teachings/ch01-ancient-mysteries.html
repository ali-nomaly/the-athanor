The Ancient Mysteries
& Secret Societies

Which Have Influenced Modern Masonic Symbolism

DESCEND

Before the profane could hope to receive the sacred fire, they were required to undergo trials of initiation whose severity tested the very limits of mortal endurance. The Mystery Schools of the ancient world — from the sweltering temples of Egypt to the oak groves of the Druids — held a single conviction: that the universe concealed, within its visible face, a hidden order accessible only to the purified mind.

Hall illuminates how these secret brotherhoods formed an unbroken chain of esoteric transmission, threading through Druidic Britain, Mithraic Persia, Gnostic Alexandria, and ultimately into the symbolic architecture of Freemasonry itself. The profane world changed; the inner doctrine, whispered between initiates, did not.

He who knows all that can be known by intellectual energy is a God among men — for his being partakes of the luminosity with which his reason has brought him into proximity.

— Manly P. Hall, Introduction

The Mystery Schools

The Druids formed the intellectual class of the Celtic world — philosopher-priests who memorized vast bodies of sacred knowledge over twenty years of oral study. They taught the immortality of the soul and the transmigration into successive lives. Their sacred groves (nemeton) served as outdoor temples. Celsus, the Roman philosopher, drew pointed comparisons between their secret doctrines and those of Egypt and Persia.
Mithraism swept the Roman legions in the 1st–4th centuries CE. Its seven grades of initiation — Raven, Occult, Soldier, Lion, Persian, Sun-Courier, Father — mirrored cosmic ascent through the planetary spheres. The sacramental meal of bread and wine, the December 25th birth date, the resurrection symbolism — all of it predated Christianity by centuries. Hall suggests the early Church absorbed these forms rather than invented them.
The philosopher Celsus (2nd century CE) wrote extensively comparing the Christian Mysteries to those of Mithras, Osiris, and Dionysus. He argued that Christianity had simply repackaged older esoteric traditions for mass consumption — stripping out the deeper philosophical core and replacing wisdom with blind faith. Hall treats Celsus as an important witness to the esoteric dimension that the institutional Church suppressed.
Hall's central thesis in this chapter is that the Mystery School tradition never died — it went underground. Through Gnostic brotherhoods, the Knights Templar, Rosicrucians, and finally Freemasonry, a lineage of initiatic knowledge passed from hand to hand. The symbols changed; the inner doctrine of cosmic law, self-purification, and ascent toward the divine remained constant across millennia.

A Lineage of Secrets

c. 3000 BCE

Egyptian Mystery Schools

The temples of Karnak and Luxor housed initiatic chambers where candidates faced trials of earth, water, fire, and air before receiving secret names of the divine.

c. 600 BCE

Eleusinian Mysteries

Greece's most sacred rites, held annually at Eleusis for 2,000 years. Initiates — from Plato to Cicero — refused to reveal what they witnessed, yet agreed it transformed the soul entirely.

c. 300 BCE

Druidic Orders of Gaul

Celtic philosopher-priests taught a doctrine of immortality, cosmic cycles, and numerology. Caesar noted they memorized thousands of sacred verses — never committing doctrine to writing.

c. 100 CE

Mithraic Legions

The cult of Mithras spread through Rome's military. Subterranean mithraeum temples were carved across the Empire from Britain to Syria, each housing seven-grade initiations under the eternal sun.

c. 1300 CE

Knights Templar & Gothic Builders

The Dionysiac Architects and later the Templars carried esoteric geometry into European cathedral building. Their suppression in 1307 forced the stream underground — into operative masonry.

1717 CE

Grand Lodge of England

Speculative Freemasonry formally organized in London — the mystery tradition donned the costume of gentleman's fraternity, preserving through allegory what could no longer be taught directly.

Ancient systems of education differed fundamentally from modern methods. The goal was not to fill the mind with information, but to awaken within the candidate the divine potentialities of his own nature.

— Manly P. Hall, Chapter I
