𓂀

No figure in the entire esoteric tradition is more ancient, more mysterious, or more consequential than Hermes Trismegistus — "Thrice-Greatest Hermes." He is simultaneously a god, a deified man, and a title assumed by a lineage of initiated priest-philosophers. The Greeks identified him with their messenger-god Hermes; the Egyptians knew him as Thoth, the ibis-headed scribe of the gods; the Arabs called him Idris; the Hebrews, Enoch. All traditions agree: this being possessed the totality of divine knowledge and transmitted it to humanity.

Hall navigates the scholarly debate with characteristic breadth: whether Hermes was one historical person who lived in Egypt circa 3000 BCE, a succession of sages who each took the title, or purely a mythological composite — the Hermetic writings themselves are what matter. In their fractured, partially translated, endlessly copied state, they contain what Hall calls "the most complete system of philosophic thought ever committed to writing in the Western world."

Who Was Hermes Trismegistus?

𓅝

EGYPT · c. 3100 BCE

Thoth, Scribe of the Gods

The ibis-headed god of writing, wisdom, magic, and the moon. Thoth weighed the souls of the dead against the feather of Ma'at in the Hall of Two Truths. He invented writing, mathematics, astronomy, and medicine — and recorded the judgments of the divine council. The Egyptians credited him with authoring 42 books of divine knowledge, each encoding a branch of the sacred sciences.

☿

GREECE · HELLENISTIC PERIOD

Hermes, Messenger of the Gods

When Alexander's armies absorbed Egypt, Greek translators identified Thoth with their own Hermes — divine messenger, guide of souls to the underworld, patron of travelers, thieves, and alchemists. The name "Trismegistus" (Thrice-Greatest) was a Greek honorific, possibly referring to his supremacy in the three domains of philosophy, priesthood, and kingship. The Corpus Hermeticum was composed in Greek but claims to transmit Egyptian teachings of immense antiquity.

✡

HEBREW TRADITION

Enoch, Who Walked With God

The Book of Genesis describes Enoch as a man who "walked with God and was not, for God took him" — meaning he did not die but was translated directly into the divine realm. Jewish and early Christian tradition credited Enoch with receiving and transmitting the same knowledge as Hermes. The apocryphal Books of Enoch describe his astronomical and angelic visions with a richness remarkably parallel to the Hermetic texts. Hall treats Hermes and Enoch as titles pointing to the same office.

☽

ISLAMIC TRADITION

Idris, the Learned One

Islamic philosophy identifies Hermes Trismegistus with the Quranic prophet Idris, who was "raised to a high station" by God. Arab alchemists and philosophers of the medieval Islamic golden age transmitted the Hermetic texts and built upon them, producing the rich tradition of Islamic alchemy (from which the word "alchemy" itself derives — al-kimiya). The Sufi orders preserved Hermetic cosmology within their mystical framework. Hall sees Islam as a crucial link in the chain of Hermetic transmission.

⚕️

HALL'S SYNTHESIS

A Succession of Initiated Sages

Hall's most nuanced reading: "Hermes Trismegistus" was not one man but a title — like "Pharaoh" or "Caesar" — assumed by successive generations of initiated Egyptian priest-philosophers who had each attained the highest degree of sacred wisdom. Each bearer added to the Hermetic corpus, expanding and deepening the tradition. The texts we possess are thus a layered transmission spanning perhaps three thousand years, all sheltering under the same divine name to claim unbroken apostolic succession from the gods themselves.

The Vision of Poimandres

The Poimandres — "Shepherd of Men" — is the first and most important text of the Corpus Hermeticum. In it, Hermes receives a divine vision in which the Universal Mind itself appears to him and reveals the complete cosmology of creation, the soul's descent, and the path of return. Hall calls it "the most sublime spiritual document in the Western philosophical tradition."

🌫️

The Infinite Light Appears

Hermes falls into a trance and encounters a boundless, limitless light — serene, joyful, filled with beauty. Then a darkness descends, coiling and serpentine, forming a kind of moist nature from which smoke and a sound arise.

🔥

The Word Descends

From the Light a holy Word descends into the moist nature. The Word is the Son of God — pure fire, leaping up, ascending. What remained was the lower nature of matter, mingled with fire and water — the seed of our world.

🌍

The Seven Governors Are Set

The Divine Mind forms seven Administrators (the planetary intelligences) who encircle the sensory world in circles and govern Fate. Their administration is called Destiny — the engine of karma and the mechanism of the soul's education through incarnation.

👁️

Man Falls and Sees His Own Reflection

The Primal Man, gazing downward through the spheres, falls in love with his own reflection in the waters of Nature and descends into matter. This is the original fall — not of sin but of fascination. The soul becomes entangled in the material world through love of its own image.

⬆️

The Path of Return

At death, the soul ascends back through the seven planetary spheres, surrendering at each gate the qualities it acquired on the way down — irrationality, desire, lust, pride, rashness, falsehood, illusion — until it enters the eighth sphere naked, possessing only its own true power, and rises into the divine.

✨

Gnosis Is the Way

The Poimandres ends with Hermes receiving his commission: go forth and teach. Those who have gnosis — direct experiential knowledge of the Divine Mind — are already saved. Those who remain in ignorance are already dying. Knowledge is the only redemption; ignorance is the only sin.

The Seven Governors of the World

The Hermetic cosmology placed seven planetary intelligences between the Divine Mind and the material world. Each Governor rules a sphere and imprints souls on their descent into matter. Hover each planet to receive its Hermetic teaching.

The Emerald Tablet of Hermes

The Tabula Smaragdina — the Emerald Tablet — is the most condensed expression of Hermetic philosophy ever written. Thirteen sentences. Newton translated it. Every alchemist memorized it. Every Rosicrucian meditated upon it. Hall calls it "the key to all magic, all alchemy, all philosophy." Hover each verse to contemplate its meaning.

True it is, without falsehood, certain and most true. That which is above is like to that which is below, and that which is below is like to that which is above, to accomplish the miracles of one thing.   And as all things were by contemplation of one, so all things arose from this one thing by a single act of adaptation.   The father thereof is the Sun, the mother the Moon. The wind carried it in its womb, the earth is the nurse thereof.   It is the father of all works of wonder throughout the whole world. The power thereof is perfect.   Separate thou the earth from the fire, the subtle from the gross, sweetly, with great industry.   It ascends from the earth to the heaven and again it descends to the earth, and receives the force of things superior and inferior.   By this means you shall have the glory of the whole world. Therefore let all obscurity flee before you.   This is the strong force of all forces, overcoming every subtle and penetrating every solid thing.   So the world was created. Hence are all wonderful adaptations, of which this is the manner. Therefore am I called Thrice Greatest Hermes, having the three parts of the philosophy of the whole world.   That which I have said of the operation of the Sun is accomplished and ended.

The Book of Thoth

Hall describes a legendary text — the original Book of Thoth — supposedly containing 36,000 volumes of divine knowledge, locked in a golden box in an Egyptian temple. No scholar has ever seen it. But its shadow falls across every esoteric tradition.

The Egyptian priest Manetho described 42 sacred books of Hermes — texts containing hymns to the gods, royal lists, ritual procedures, geographical knowledge, priestly education, astronomy, music, writing, medicine, and more. These 42 books were the complete curriculum of the Egyptian temple school. Most were lost in the fires of Alexandria. Hall believes fragments survived in the Hermetic corpus and in early Gnostic texts.
The collection of 17 Greek texts rediscovered in 1460 CE and translated by Ficino for Cosimo de' Medici — who ordered Ficino to set aside his Plato translations to do this one first. The texts caused a sensation in Renaissance Florence: here, apparently, was Greek philosophy predating Plato and Pythagoras, teaching many of the same doctrines. Modern scholars date the texts to the 2nd–3rd centuries CE, but Hall holds their content reflects a much older oral tradition.
The Emerald Tablet's closing claim that Hermes possesses "the three parts of the philosophy of the whole world" refers to: (1) Theurgy — the science of working with divine forces; (2) Astrology — the science of understanding those forces as they manifest through celestial mechanics; (3) Alchemy — the science of transforming matter and soul by applying the same principles at the material level. These three are not separate disciplines but one unified science of transformation at different scales.
Drawing on the Hermetic tradition, Hall outlines seven universal principles: Mentalism (all is mind), Correspondence (as above so below), Vibration (nothing rests), Polarity (opposites are the same), Rhythm (everything has tides), Cause and Effect (every effect has its cause), and Gender (gender is in everything). These seven principles, he argues, if truly understood and applied, would give their possessor mastery over all levels of existence.
